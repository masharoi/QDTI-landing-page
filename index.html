Quantum Diamond Technologies

QDTI is a technology company based in Somerville, Massachusetts, and seeking to bring a new set of technologies from the lab to commercial application. Founded with a team of leading quantum scientists from Harvard University, QDTI is commercializing a new class of nanoscale devices that rely on the fundamental properties of quantum mechanics to allow new applications in sensing, metrology, computing and other fields.

Magnetic Imaging Platform

QDTI uses NV quantum sensing in a wide-field magnetic imaging modality, a versatile approach that can be applied to a wide range of magnetic samples. Wide-field quantum magnetic imaging simultaneously interrogates millions of individual pixels with sub-micron spatial resolution over a field of view that may exceed 1 mm.

NV quantum magnetic sensing is performed by observing spin-dependent fluorescence from NV quantum sensors. When excited with green light from the ground electronic state, NV centers emit red fluorescence. This property allows for optically detected magnetic resonance (ODMR), in which the NV center’s magnetic environment is measured optically by observing its quantum state change in response to resonant microwave fields.

The imagining platform contains NV centers in a thin sheet at the surface of a millimeter-scale diamond sensor. The NV dependent fluorescence is imaged using conventional microscope optics. When a magnetic sample is introduced to the sensor surface, each NV center experiences a magnetic (Zeeman) shift in response to its local magnetic field. A microwave field is applied to the entire sensor to drive spin transitions in the NV centers, then collect fluorescence images while the microwave frequency is swept. As each individual NV center comes into resonance with the microwave drive, its reduced fluorescence rate causes the image to dim in that pixel. By acquiring several images at a range of frequencies and performing an analysis at each pixel, we generate a map of the magnetic field in the sensing plane.

Quantum Sensor Technology

QDTI’s quantum magnetic sensing technology is based upon crystal defects in diamond called nitrogen-vacancy (NV) centers. NV centers result from the substitution of two adjacent carbon atoms in the diamond lattice with a nitrogen atom and an empty site (a vacancy). Each NV center has an electronic spin and a strong magnetic moment that provides sensitivity to magnetic fields. Pioneering research at Harvard established the superior magnetic sensitivity of NV quantum sensors in diamond compared to other technologies, particularly for high resolution sensing applications. Recent demonstrations of the tremendous sensitivity afforded by NV center magnetometers include sub-nanometer magnetic localization of individual proton spins, single-molecule detection, label-free magnetic resonance identification of individual protein molecules, and magnetic measurement of neuron action potentials from the exterior of whole, live marine worms. Diamond NV magnetometry has in recent years moved beyond demonstration and into increasing use as a precision tool across many fields. A wide-field imaging modality can simultaneously interrogate fields of view larger than 1 mm with sub-micron spatial resolution. Wide field quantum magnetic imaging systems are now in regular research use for biological and geophysical magnetic studies, testament to the versatility of this technology.

Why QDTI?

Detecting tiny concentrations of biomolecules such as proteins is complicated by the vast quantity of other material in complex biofluids such as blood or serum. Industry standard high-sensitivity assays rely on measuring light emitted from a fluorescent molecule label, or from the product of an enzyme label on a substrate. Filter leakage, detector noise, and nonspecific autofluorescence of the sample itself all produce backgrounds that compete with true signals. Ultrasensitive assays must correctly identify individual target molecules amid millions or billions of others, placing strict constraints on false positive signals. Technologies based on optical detection therefore require lengthy sample preparation to reduce backgrounds, necessitating large, complex, and expensive instruments. QDTI’s magnetic detection, in contrast, provides high sensitivity even in raw samples. Biofluids are negligibly magnetic compared to magnetic particle labels, so there is no intrinsic sample background — the magnetic signal comes only from the labels.
